Microsoft 365 Exchange Online Migration

Exchange Online Migration: Lessons Learned

Alex Dai· 2026-02-28· 10 min read

Migrating an organization's email infrastructure to Exchange Online is one of the most user-visible IT projects you can undertake. When WCB-Alberta moved its on-premises Exchange environment to Microsoft 365, the project touched every single employee. This article covers the decisions, complications, and hard-won lessons from that migration.

💡 This article assumes familiarity with Active Directory, Exchange Server, and basic Microsoft 365 concepts. Our environment was Exchange Server 2016 with a hybrid configuration target.

Migration Approach: Why Hybrid?

There are three common approaches to Exchange Online migration: cutover (move everything at once), staged (batches over time), and hybrid (coexistence with on-premises). We chose a hybrid migration for several reasons:

  • Our user base was large enough that a cutover would create unacceptable downtime risk
  • Certain mailboxes had compliance holds that required careful handling during transition
  • We wanted to preserve full free/busy calendar sharing during the transition period
  • Some shared mailboxes and distribution groups needed to remain on-premises temporarily

The hybrid approach adds complexity — you're essentially running two environments simultaneously — but it gives you the flexibility to migrate at a controlled pace and roll back individual mailboxes if issues arise.

Step 1: Pre-Migration Assessment

The assessment phase is where you avoid the most painful surprises. Key things to inventory:

  • Mailbox sizes and item counts — Large mailboxes (we had several over 50GB) take significantly longer to migrate and are more prone to failures
  • Mailbox types — User, shared, room, equipment, and public folders each have different migration paths
  • Client applications — Any application sending email via SMTP relay or using EWS/MAPI directly needs to be updated
  • Third-party integrations — Fax-to-email, HR systems, ticketing systems that use email authentication
  • Distribution groups and mail-enabled objects — These need to be synchronized to Azure AD before mailboxes can be migrated

We used the Microsoft 365 Assessment Tool alongside a custom PowerShell script to generate our inventory. Don't skip this step — we found 14 service accounts sending email that nobody had documented.

Step 2: Hybrid Configuration Wizard (HCW)

The Hybrid Configuration Wizard automates most of the Exchange hybrid setup, but "most" is doing a lot of work in that sentence. Common issues we hit:

  • Certificate mismatches: Your on-premises Exchange server's SSL certificate must be trusted and match the FQDN used for Autodiscover and mail flow. Expired or self-signed certs will break the HCW.
  • Firewall rules: Port 443 outbound to Microsoft 365 endpoints needs to be open. Many organizations have fine-grained egress controls that require specific allow-list entries.
  • AAD Connect sync scope: Mail-enabled objects must be in scope for Azure AD Connect sync. We had a few OUs excluded that needed to be added before their mailboxes could be migrated.

Run the HCW from a server with direct internet access if possible — proxy complications can cause cryptic errors that are hard to trace.

Step 3: Mail Flow Considerations

During hybrid coexistence, you have two choices for mail flow: centralized (all inbound/outbound goes through on-premises) or decentralized (cloud mailboxes route directly). We started with centralized to maintain our existing email security gateway, then switched to decentralized once we validated Defender for Office 365 coverage.

Key things to test before starting wave migrations:

  • Internal mail routing between on-prem and cloud mailboxes (both directions)
  • Calendar free/busy lookups across the hybrid boundary
  • Meeting acceptance and updates from mixed environments
  • Shared mailbox access from Outlook and OWA
  • SMTP relay from internal applications

The Biggest Surprises

  • Public folders: We had legacy public folders that hadn't been touched in years but had active users. Migrating these to Exchange Online public folders (or replacing them with SharePoint/Teams) required significant planning and stakeholder communication.
  • Outlook profile updates: Even with Autodiscover configured correctly, some users with older Outlook versions needed manual profile recreation after their mailbox moved. We built this into our helpdesk runbook.
  • Litigation hold and eDiscovery: Mailboxes with compliance holds had to be kept on-premises until legal confirmed the cloud equivalent (Microsoft Purview) was configured and validated. This delayed our decommission timeline by six weeks.
  • Mobile device re-enrollment: MDM-managed devices picked up the new mail profile automatically, but BYOD devices required users to remove and re-add their work account. Communicating this clearly in advance prevented most helpdesk tickets.

What Went Well

The wave-based approach was the right call. By migrating department by department (starting with IT, then smaller departments, saving the largest last), we refined our runbook with each wave. By wave 3, migrations that initially took 4 hours of active support had become nearly self-service.

User communication was the highest-leverage investment. We sent a prep email 5 days before, a "migration tonight" reminder the day before, and a "what to do Monday morning" guide the morning after. Departments that received this full communication package generated roughly 60% fewer helpdesk tickets than our first wave where we under-communicated.

Final Thoughts

Exchange Online migration is a mature, well-documented process — Microsoft has done this thousands of times. The technical steps are manageable. The harder parts are the organizational ones: stakeholder alignment, user communication, and dealing with the legacy integrations that nobody documented. Invest time upfront in the assessment phase, and treat change management as a first-class workstream, not an afterthought.
