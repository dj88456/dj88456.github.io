From IT Specialist to IT Architect: My Career Journey

Alex Dai· 2025-12-10· 6 min read

I graduated with a B.Sc. in Computing Science from the University of Alberta in 2017 with a vague plan of "working in tech." Eight years later, I'm designing enterprise cloud architectures for a major provincial organization. This is the honest account of how that happened — the deliberate moves, the lucky breaks, and the things I'd do differently.

💡 This is a personal reflection, not a prescriptive career guide. Career paths in IT are genuinely diverse. I'm sharing what worked for me, not a universal formula.

The Early Years: Building a Foundation

My first real IT role after university was IT Team Lead at Sunrise Health Lodge, a long-term care facility. In hindsight, this was an ideal first job for someone who wanted to eventually move into architecture: it was a small team, which meant wearing many hats.

In two years I touched networking, server administration, desktop support, vendor management, and project management. None of it at a deep level, but I got a breadth of exposure that I've drawn on ever since. Generalist roles early in your career are underrated. Specialists earn more at the junior level, but generalists develop pattern recognition across domains that becomes increasingly valuable as you move toward architecture roles.

The biggest technical skill I built in that period was PowerShell. Automating repetitive tasks — user provisioning, printer deployments, backup verification — was less about the scripts themselves and more about developing the mindset of: "if I'm doing this manually three times, I should automate it."

Moving to WCB-Alberta: Scale Changes Everything

Joining WCB-Alberta as an IT Specialist I in 2019 was a significant jump in organizational scale. Where my previous employer had a small headcount, WCB has over 1,500 employees, with offices across Alberta and complex infrastructure spanning multiple sites.

The first thing I noticed: the problems at scale are qualitatively different, not just quantitatively larger. At a small organization, you can often solve problems by knowing every system and every person. At scale, you need processes, documentation, and architecture — because no single person can hold the whole environment in their head.

The Transition to Architecture

The jump from IT Specialist II to IT Architect was the most significant transition of my career. The work changed from implementing solutions to designing them. A few things made that transition possible:

Certifications as structured learning

I'm not a certification maximalist — I don't think a wall of badges translates directly to competence. But studying for the Azure Solutions Architect Expert (AZ-305) forced me to engage with architectural concepts I hadn't encountered in specialist work: hub-and-spoke networking, management group hierarchies, governance at scale. The certification was a structured curriculum that accelerated my ramp-up into architecture thinking.

Leading a significant project

The Dynamics 365 deployment I led as a Specialist gave me credibility and visibility. It wasn't an architecture role, but driving a cross-functional project end-to-end demonstrated the skills that architecture requires: stakeholder management, requirement gathering, trade-off analysis, documentation. When the IT Architect role opened, I had a concrete body of work to point to.

Finding a mentor in the architecture space

I was fortunate to work alongside a senior enterprise architect who was willing to explain the "why" behind design decisions, not just the "what." Learning to think in terms of constraints and trade-offs — rather than just technical best practices — was the most important intellectual shift of my career.

What I'd Tell My Earlier Self

  • Write more. Documentation, design documents, post-mortems. Writing forces clarity of thinking, and a well-documented project is evidence of competence in a way that undocumented work never is.
  • Learn the business context. Understanding why WCB needs specific compliance controls, or what the operational impact of downtime is, made me a significantly better architect than pure technical depth alone.
  • Don't neglect soft skills. The ability to explain a complex technical decision to a non-technical stakeholder has been more career-relevant than almost any specific technical skill.
  • Certifications are a tool, not a goal. Study for them to learn, not to accumulate. The ones where I genuinely engaged with the material paid dividends; the ones I crammed through didn't stick.

Where Things Stand Now

As IT Architect II at WCB-Alberta, my focus has expanded to multi-site infrastructure strategy and Power Platform governance. The problems are larger and more ambiguous than anything I dealt with as a Specialist, which is exactly what I was looking for.

The honest truth is that "IT Architect" is a broad title and the skills it requires are never fully acquired — the technology landscape shifts too fast. The most useful thing I've developed isn't any specific technical skill; it's the ability to rapidly come up to speed on new domains and reason about trade-offs under uncertainty. That capacity, more than any certification or technology expertise, is what I expect to carry forward.
