Designing an Enterprise Azure Landing Zone from Scratch

Alex Dai· 2026-03-15· 8 min read

When WCB-Alberta decided to move to the cloud, I was tasked with designing and implementing our first Azure tenant and landing zone from scratch. This article summarizes the key decisions, pitfalls, and lessons learned from that journey.

💡 This article is aimed at IT architects and cloud engineers planning their first enterprise Azure deployment. Familiarity with Azure basics is assumed.

What Is an Azure Landing Zone?

An Azure Landing Zone is a scalable, secure, and governed environment that serves as the foundation for all your cloud workloads. Think of it as the blueprint for your Azure infrastructure — it defines how subscriptions are organized, how networking is structured, who can access what, and how policies are enforced.

Microsoft's Cloud Adoption Framework (CAF) provides the reference architecture, but every organization needs to adapt it to their specific compliance requirements, existing infrastructure, and team capabilities.

Step 1: Subscription and Management Group Design

The first major decision is how to organize your subscriptions. We chose a platform/application separation model:

  • Platform subscriptions — Identity, Management, Connectivity (hub network, DNS, firewall)
  • Landing zone subscriptions — Corp (internal apps), Online (public-facing), Sandbox (dev/test)

A common mistake is cramming everything into a single subscription early on. This works fine at first but becomes a governance nightmare as teams and workloads grow. Designing for separation from day one saves significant pain later.

Step 2: Networking Architecture

We adopted a hub-and-spoke topology with a central hub virtual network hosting shared services (Azure Firewall, VPN Gateway, DNS). Spoke VNets for each workload connect to the hub via VNet peering.

Key decisions we made:

  • Use Azure Firewall for east-west and north-south traffic inspection
  • Dedicated DNS private zones for internal name resolution
  • ExpressRoute for hybrid connectivity to on-premises (WCB has significant on-prem infrastructure)
  • Private Endpoints for PaaS services — no public exposure for storage, SQL, Key Vault

Step 3: Identity and RBAC

We integrated Azure AD with our on-premises Active Directory via Azure AD Connect. For RBAC, we followed the principle of least privilege with a tiered role model:

  • Platform team — Owner on platform subscriptions, Contributor on shared services
  • Application teams — Contributor scoped to their own resource groups
  • Read-only groups — Auditors, security team, senior management

We created custom roles for specific scenarios (e.g., a "Network Contributor" role that can manage NICs and NSGs but not create VNets). Document your role definitions carefully — they become the foundation of your security posture.

Step 4: Policy as Code

Azure Policy is one of the most powerful governance tools available, and also one of the most under-used. We implemented policies to:

  • Enforce allowed resource locations (Canada Central and Canada East only)
  • Require specific tags on all resources (CostCenter, Owner, Environment)
  • Deny public IP addresses on VMs in production
  • Require diagnostic settings to be sent to our central Log Analytics workspace

Start with Audit mode before switching to Deny — you'll almost certainly find existing resources that violate your policies, and you need time to remediate them.

The Biggest Pitfalls

  • Under-estimating hybrid connectivity complexity: VPN/ExpressRoute setup with on-prem firewalls took twice as long as planned
  • Not involving security early enough: Retrofitting security controls is far more painful than designing them in from the start
  • Treating it as a one-time project: A landing zone is a living platform — plan for ongoing maintenance, updates, and team enablement

Final Thoughts

A well-designed landing zone is an investment that pays dividends every time a new workload is onboarded. The goal isn't perfection on day one — it's building a solid, extensible foundation that your teams can build on with confidence. Start with the non-negotiables (identity, networking, governance), then iterate.
